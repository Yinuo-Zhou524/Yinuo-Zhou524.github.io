# Research

My research focuses on tree physiology and forest health, especially how bark and other non-foliar tissues contribute to plant water relations under environmental stress. I am interested in how residual water loss, bark structure, and environmental sensitivity influence tree function across flooding, heat, and drought conditions. My work combines field studies, greenhouse experiments, controlled laboratory measurements, bark anatomy, and quantitative modeling to better understand how woody tissues respond to changing environments.

## Research Questions

My work is guided by several broad questions:

- How variable is bark water vapor conductance among tree species and within individual trees?
- How do flooding, temperature, and mechanical disturbance alter bark function?
- What bark traits, such as thickness and lenticel characteristics, help explain variation in bark conductance?
- Can non-destructive approaches such as hyperspectral reflectance be used to estimate residual water-loss traits?
- How might bark-mediated residual water loss influence tree stress, survival, and mortality under climate extremes?

## Bark Water Vapor Conductance Across Species and Environments

A central theme of my research is bark water vapor conductance (*g*<sub>bark</sub>), a residual water-loss pathway that remains active when stomata are absent or closed. Although leaves are the primary organs of gas exchange, stems also exchange water vapor and carbon dioxide, and bark permeability may influence whole-plant water balance under stress.

My work has shown that *g*<sub>bark</sub> varies substantially among temperate forest tree species and responds dynamically to environmental conditions rather than behaving as a fixed trait. In previous experiments, I found that flooding reduced bark water vapor conductance, while stem bending increased it, showing that bark permeability is sensitive to both environmental and physical disturbance.

## Temperature Sensitivity of Residual Water Loss Through Bark

Another major part of my research examines how bark water vapor conductance responds to temperature across species and seasons. This work addresses an important gap in plant water relations, because many current frameworks for tree drought and heat stress emphasize leaf and xylem processes while giving much less attention to dynamic variation in bark-mediated residual water loss.

Using controlled temperature treatments, I measure *g*<sub>bark</sub> from low to very high temperatures and compare responses among temperate tree species. I am particularly interested in whether warming changes bark resistance to vapor diffusion, whether these responses differ among species, and how bark traits such as bark thickness ratio, lenticel size, and lenticel density relate to those patterns.

## Flooding, Lenticels, and Bark Function

I am also developing research on how flooding alters bark structure and function over time. Flooding can induce lenticel hypertrophy in some woody species, but the functional consequences of these changes for bark permeability and water loss remain poorly understood.

My greenhouse flooding research examines whether chronic flooding changes lenticel morphology, bark water vapor conductance, and related traits across species that differ in flood tolerance. This work links visible bark responses with functional changes in water vapor exchange and broader stress physiology.

## Hyperspectral Reflectance and Non-destructive Trait Estimation

Another part of my research examines whether hyperspectral reflectance can be used to estimate bark and leaf traits related to residual water loss. This project is motivated by the difficulty of measuring many functional traits using destructive and labor-intensive methods, especially across large numbers of trees and species. Hyperspectral approaches offer the possibility of rapid, non-destructive trait estimation and may help connect bark physiology with scalable monitoring tools. :contentReference[oaicite:1]{index=1}

In this work, I evaluate whether spectral reflectance can predict traits including bark water vapor conductance, bark water content, bark density, bark thickness-related traits, bark texture, and leaf minimum conductance. I compare statistical and machine-learning approaches such as partial least squares regression, Random Forest, and XGBoost. Preliminary results suggest that hyperspectral models perform well for several structural traits, including outer bark thickness and outer bark thickness ratio, and very well for leaf water content, but perform poorly for *g*<sub>bark</sub>. Rather than a failure, this result suggests that some bark functional traits may not have a strong direct spectral signature, which is itself informative for how bark physiology can and cannot be scaled using remote sensing tools. :contentReference[oaicite:2]{index=2}

## Within-tree and Localized Variation in Bark Conductance

In addition to species-level and environmental comparisons, I am interested in small-scale variation in bark function within individual trees. This includes how bark conductance changes along stems and branches and how localized bark injury may alter water loss during damage and healing.

These questions are motivated by the idea that bark function is spatially heterogeneous and that small-scale structural variation may have important implications for whole-plant water balance.

## Approaches

My research integrates several complementary approaches:

- controlled laboratory measurements of bark water vapor conductance
- greenhouse flooding experiments
- field sampling and field-based measurements
- bark anatomical measurements, including bark thickness and lenticel traits
- hyperspectral reflectance and trait modeling
- statistical and machine-learning analysis in R and Python

## Broader Goals

More broadly, my research aims to improve understanding of how residual water-loss pathways contribute to tree responses to environmental stress. By connecting bark structure, bark conductance, environmental sensitivity, and scalable measurement approaches, I hope to clarify how woody tissues influence tree resilience under climate extremes.
