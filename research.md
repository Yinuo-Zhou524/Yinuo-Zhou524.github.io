# Research

My research focuses on tree physiology and forest health, especially how bark and other non-foliar tissues contribute to plant water relations under environmental stress. I am interested in how residual water loss, bark structure, and environmental sensitivity influence tree function across flooding, heat, and drought conditions. My work combines field studies, greenhouse experiments, controlled laboratory measurements, and quantitative analysis to better understand how woody tissues respond to changing environments.

## Research Questions

My work is guided by several broad questions:

- How variable is bark water vapor conductance among tree species and within individual trees?
- How do flooding, temperature, and mechanical disturbance alter bark function?
- What anatomical traits, such as bark thickness and lenticel characteristics, help explain variation in bark conductance?
- How might bark-mediated residual water loss influence tree stress, survival, and mortality under extreme conditions?

## Bark Water Vapor Conductance Across Species and Environments

A central theme of my research is bark water vapor conductance (*g*<sub>bark</sub>), a residual water-loss pathway that remains active when stomata are absent or closed. Although leaves are the main organs of plant gas exchange, stems also exchange water vapor and carbon dioxide, and bark permeability may influence whole-plant water balance under stress.

My work has shown that *g*<sub>bark</sub> varies substantially among temperate forest tree species and responds dynamically to environmental conditions rather than behaving as a fixed trait. In previous experiments, I found that flooding reduced bark water vapor conductance, while stem bending increased it, showing that bark permeability is sensitive to both environmental and physical disturbance.

## Temperature Sensitivity of Residual Water Loss Through Bark

Another major part of my research examines how bark water vapor conductance responds to temperature across species and seasons. This work addresses an important gap in plant water relations, because many current frameworks for tree drought and heat stress emphasize leaf and xylem processes while giving much less attention to dynamic variation in bark-mediated residual water loss.

Using controlled temperature treatments, I measure *g*<sub>bark</sub> from low to very high temperatures and compare responses among temperate tree species. I am particularly interested in whether warming changes bark resistance to vapor diffusion, whether these responses differ among species, and how bark traits such as bark thickness ratio, lenticel size, and lenticel density relate to those patterns. This work connects bark physiology to broader questions about heat stress, seasonal acclimation, and the representation of residual water loss in plant hydraulic models.

## Flooding, Lenticels, and Bark Function

I am also developing research on how flooding alters bark structure and function over time. Flooding can induce lenticel hypertrophy in some woody species, but the implications of these structural changes for bark permeability and water loss remain poorly understood.

My greenhouse flooding experiment examines whether chronic flooding changes lenticel morphology, bark water vapor conductance, and associated traits across species that differ in flood tolerance. This work is designed to link visible bark responses, such as hypertrophied lenticels, with functional changes in water vapor exchange and broader stress physiology.

## Within-Tree and Localized Variation in Bark Conductance

In addition to species-level and environmental comparisons, I am interested in small-scale variation in bark function within individual trees. This includes how bark conductance changes along stems and branches and how localized bark injury may alter water loss during damage and healing.

These questions are motivated by the idea that bark function is spatially heterogeneous and that small-scale structural variation may have important implications for whole-plant water balance. My developing work on bark damage and recovery is aimed at understanding how injury affects bark permeability through time and how healing may restore resistance to water loss.

## Approaches

My research integrates several complementary approaches:

- controlled laboratory measurements of bark water vapor conductance
- greenhouse flooding experiments
- field sampling and field-based measurements
- bark anatomical measurements, including bark thickness and lenticel traits
- dendrochronological and ecophysiological perspectives on tree stress
- quantitative analysis in R and related statistical tools

## Broader Goals

More broadly, my research aims to improve understanding of how residual water-loss pathways contribute to tree responses to environmental stress. By connecting bark structure, bark conductance, and stress physiology, I hope to help clarify how woody tissues influence tree resilience under climate extremes.
